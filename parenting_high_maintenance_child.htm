Parenting High Maintenance Children

Most parents question their parenting success at times, usually more towards the end of the day. It is amazing how a healthy six year old can be just that age in the morning and only two or three by the end of the long and hard parenting day. Regression is not a fancy psychological concept, it happens every day in almost every household: children no longer act their age and if the Parenting Peak-hours are really bad; neither do their parents. These Parenting Peak-hours differ from one household to the next but getting-the-kids-to-get-dressed-to-go-to-school, getting- them-to-stop-fighting-with-each-other and getting-them-to-do-their-homework must easily be near the top of the least favourite parenting activities. Just as getting them to have a bath, eat healthily and go to bed at a reasonable time is too. Some parents feel that the Parenting Peak-hours never cease.  

The trouble with children

The trouble with children is that they really are highly unprofessional; they always let emotions get in the way of a sensibly planned parenting working day. If only children would respond to our polite requests for co-operation with: "yes, of course Daddy, I'll do it immediately" or "thank you for your wisdom Mother, it is really very late and of course I should have been asleep by now". Children in general don't respond all that positively to suggestions or instructions that are contrary to their own plans. Often one hears about children who "don’t listen", which on closer inspection refers not to their auditory skills but to the fact that they don’t choose to co-operate!! Interestingly the parental presumption seems to be that as long as they decode our verbal communications successfully, they will naturally comply with our suggestions. The question is can we expect our children to always accept our plans?  It is not a child's ultimate fantasy to be co-operating with his/her parents on a sensible basis. It is a parent's ultimate fantasy that s/he does.

Often we like to work with our children on a basis of mutual co-operation. This is where we reach ‘agreements” with our children; they seem to consent to go along with our sensibly formulated plans in a happy, quiet and unthinking moment. Before they know it they have agreed to a deal that when it comes to it, turns out to be only advantageous to the parent, like go to bed on time or stop annoying your sister. Agreements in general are only binding if ‘informed consent’ was secured. The younger the child the more ‘informed’ consent is un-realistic.

In reality we need children sometimes to do as we tell them to without them having the right to decline, gracefully or otherwise. How often are we negotiating and arguing with them as we would with adults or colleagues, meanwhile elevating children to a position of power that is exciting, confusing and/or frightening to them all at the same time?

Do you want to be friends with your children?

Being friends with our children is a privileged status we have to work hard for. This status will only be achieved when they are still confiding in us freely once they have their own family. Before that time we parents are in charge of the children and we should be and being in charge is mutually exclusive with being friends. Not putting ourselves in charge is like driving across the Sydney Harbour Bridge in peak-hour and allowing our child to pull on the steering wheel, while we patiently explain that this is too dangerous and that mummy or daddy really should be making the car change lanes. 

Parenting should be easy

It would seem that parenting only feels right if we are able to do this in a most comfortable and casual way. Each child borne ideally comes with a tiny remote control for us to steer the child with if it goes ever so slightly off course, because children ideally should only ever go slightly off course……. Worse again is that we feel we are somehow failing in our mission as a parent when we need to use other than subtle and nicely positive techniques.  

Children’s and parents’ privileges

Maybe at times we are expecting too much of our children. Many books and articles about Parenting are written in such a way that the ‘lay-parent’ could be forgiven for thinking that it is ultimately possible to raise children without ever witnessing tantrums, without ever having to raise one’s voice, and that it is possible to never have to firmly grab hold of your child in a heated exchange of words. Or …. if you find yourself using any of these parenting methods, you should eternally feel guilty. The truth is that it is a child's mission in life to go for what s/he can get. We can't hold that against them. However it is a Parent's privilege in life to remain sane under all that comes with this thing called parenting.

Some children can be raised with ease and how we parent them seems to come naturally. Some children require a lot more energy, patience and determination; high maintenance children. Some children require a parenting style that is far removed from how we would prefer to parent. A constant mixture of frustration, pain and guilt is felt because how we want to parent doesn't seem to work and how we are required to parent doesn’t feel good.    

Generally we can evaluate any child-parent relationship in terms of the discrepancy between what I call the Preferred Parenting Style (PPS) and the Required Parenting Style (RPS). The closer these styles the easier raising this child is experienced. The further apart two things happen: the child is experienced as harder to manage and the Actual Parenting Style (APS) will be far more fluctuating, adding to the stress already felt by the parents. To be consistent in your parenting only works well with easy children. With High Maintenance Children inconsistency is called flexible and creative parenting. Some parents feel under great pressure trying to achieve positive parenting fulfilment. In order not to compromise their Preferred Parenting Style, they may not offer their child the (high) boundaries that s/he needs. When this doesn't work and the parent has to be less "democratic" than s/he prefers to be, the child can get blamed for this. As in when the child displays unacceptable behaviour, s/he gets into trouble for both the "offence" as well as the fact that the parent feels upset because s/he needs (again!) to use a parenting style which is not preferred.

With High Maintenance children it is more important than with "other" children that both parents agree on what the Required Parenting Style is and that both parents will agree to act according to the RPS. Parenting High Maintenance children can put a strain on the marital relationship especially if the parents do not agree on the RPS. One way of dealing with this is to set up a Required Parenting Plan which states all the child's needs in terms of boundaries and rules and the required parenting responses. When both parents can agree and act on this plan the benefits will be great for everyone including the child concerned. It is important to realise that parenting is not a level playing field, it can’t be and it shouldn't be. Children depend on us for their psychological security and that means not only love but boundaries as well.

Don't think that getting-to-school-on-time is only a meaningful experience if the child has dressed him/herself, had breakfast, brushed his/her teeth and is ready to go to school on time, all of this voluntarily and without arguing with his/her siblings.

Don't wait until such time that your child ‘provokes’ you to blow up. If it is necessary to be intentional, directive and you feel you have to skip the niceties, so be it, and after all you are in charge. When you blow up when you decide to, you will find that it will be far less intense because you will stop blaming your child for your own outburst. And it will be a far less painful experience for everyone involved. 

Remember, the more you place yourself in charge, the more secure your children will feel. It is a safe feeling to know that your parents can manage anything you might throw at them! Or at least look like they can! When it is time to set boundaries be decisive and when it is time for love and affection spoil them. But don't mix the two!

Children are the most important assets one can ever dream to have. A non-punitive, leading-from-the-front style of parenting has the best chance to succeed with raising High Maintenance children.

One of the most frustrating experiences in parenting would have to be finding yourself using methods of parenting you would rather avoid. You know, when you have to cajole, yell and scream at the children before they will listen to you. Why is parenting such a demanding job? What am I doing wrong? This is a question many parents ask themselves.

 

 

Many parents want to be friends with their children. Maybe you do? Well, picture this: your best friend is coming for dinner and you have made a real effort to cook something special but she is not eating much at all and is just picking at the food. Would you raise your voice and tell her that if she doesn't finish her meal there will be no dessert? Of course you wouldn't, but you probably would tell your child.

 

 

 

Life can be seen as a park with deep ponds and patches of newly sewn grass. Some children can be guided through this park, by setting out a few stakes with a bit of string. These are the children on whom any sensible parenting style can be practised with guaranteed success. Other children will need 2 meter high steel fences to keep them of the grass and out of the water. Only firm and resolute, but not punitive parenting will stand a chance here. To parent on the principle of co-operation might work with the first child but it is usually fails with the second. 

At times this means that we might have to be far more insistent or directive than we would like to be; "Get dressed now, ....please!" sounds quite different from "Please,.....get dressed now?".

Imagine that your child is invited to a birthday party. When you arrive there, you find that chocolate crackles and carbonated, caffeine containing drinks are available in abundance. As soon as you have had a chance to say hello to the child's mother, you find yourself wondering if this is the same spotlessly clean child you left your house with because he now has spilled a drink over his lovely new outfit and his face is covered in chocolate. Next thing you know your little "friend" starts pushing the other children because he doesn't want to let go of the presents that belong to the birthday child.

The ultimate guide to the ever successful parent requires you to handle this smilingly and with little effort. You know however that the only thing that will really work is to act very decisively and to ultimately dare to take the child home even if you have only just arrived. Some parents will admire your courage, but others will think that you have just lost it completely -  your status as ever successful parent that is.

When we are called on to parent High Maintenance children we need to realise that we perhaps need to grieve for the child we didn't get. Secondly we need to recognise that we also may need to grieve for the parent we can't (afford to) be!
